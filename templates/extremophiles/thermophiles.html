Heat Adaptation

Thermophiles

Organisms adapted to high temperatures, from geothermal pools to deep-sea vents.

Pyrococcus furiosus: A fast-growing model for life at extreme heat.

Pyrococcus furiosus was discovered in 1986 by Stetter and colleagues, when archaea were often called archaebacteria. The name "furiosus" refers to its rapid movement and growth. Unlike many thermophiles, it can use starch as a food source and grows only in oxygen-free conditions. It also has an unusual glycolytic pathway. Its best-known contribution is the high-fidelity Pfu DNA polymerase, widely used in PCR because it makes fewer errors than Taq polymerase.

Geobacillus stearothermophilus: A heat-tolerant benchmark for sterilization.

Geobacillus stearothermophilus is a heat-loving, spore-forming bacterium with a complex taxonomic history. It was first grouped under Bacillus, but work by Nazina and colleagues helped define the separate Geobacillus genus. Recent studies suggest this group may still be split further, showing how difficult microbial classification can be. Beyond taxonomy, it is important in the food industry because its hardy spores can survive processing and contaminate products. For the same reason, its spores are widely used as biological indicators to confirm whether sterilization methods are effective.
